# HOME ROW — Project Context

## Core Concept

**HOME ROW** is a cursed retro typing tutor horror game built around a grounded traumatic event with ambiguous supernatural preservation.

The player finds and boots an old 1990s educational typing program called:

> **HOME ROW**  
> *A Friendly Typing Program for Growing Hands*

At first it appears to be harmless school software: cheerful mascot, beige UI, simple typing drills, classroom vocabulary, and fake Windows 95 presentation. But the program recognises the player, not by name, but by their typing rhythm.

Years ago, the player was a child in the same school computer lab where **Emily Vale** disappeared. The player did not harm Emily, but they were present. They heard something. They were told by a teacher to keep typing and not turn around. As an adult, they have suppressed the event.

HOME ROW now begins revealing the truth through corrupted lessons, hidden text, typing errors, recovered system logs, and muscle memory.

The program should remain ambiguous. It may be:

1. A haunted archive
2. Emily’s preserved final testimony
3. The player’s own suppressed memory / subconscious speaking through familiar software
4. All three at once

The grounded event remains human and institutional. The supernatural layer exists because memory, guilt, and trauma sometimes feel supernatural when they return.

---

## Emotional Core

The story is not about a spooky computer randomly haunting someone.

It is about this:

> A child witnessed another child being erased, and survived by obeying.  
> Years later, the only thing brave enough to remember is a broken typing program.

The player is morally implicated, but not monstrous. They were a frightened child under adult authority. The horror comes from obedience, institutional language, suppression, and the act of turning memory into record.

---

## Main Mystery

Emily Vale disappeared after being kept behind in the school computer lab by a teacher, **Mr. Calder**.

The official story says Emily was absent, transferred, disruptive, imaginative, or unreliable.

The hidden truth suggests:

- Emily was kept after school.
- Mr. Calder locked or controlled access to the computer lab.
- Emily tried to type an “unsanctioned” statement into HOME ROW.
- Another child was present at a second workstation.
- That second child was the player.
- The player was told to keep typing and not turn around.
- Emily disappeared from the school record.
- HOME ROW preserved fragments of the session.
- The player’s body remembers what their conscious mind suppressed.

---

## Main Characters

### The Player / Former Pupil

The player is now an adult, but as a child they were present during Emily’s final session.

Their memory is fragmented:

- Rain against the computer lab windows
- Chair scrape
- Emily breathing hard
- Mr. Calder’s shoes
- Keyboard tapping
- The command: “Keep typing.”
- The sentence: “I will not interrupt.”
- The feeling of almost turning around

The player should never be framed as the villain. They were a child trapped inside adult authority.

Their arc is from denial to partial witnesshood.

---

### Emily Vale

Emily is the missing child.

She is remembered through corrupted records, typing fragments, and hidden text. She should not appear as a simple exposition ghost. Her presence should feel incomplete, like pressure behind the screen.

Official descriptions frame her as:

- Difficult
- Imaginative
- Disruptive
- Attention-seeking
- Unreliable
- In need of firm boundaries

Hidden fragments suggest she was frightened, trying to communicate, and trying to make a record.

Possible recovered fragments:

```text
MY NAME IS EMILY VALE
I AM IN THE COMPUTER ROOM
MR CALDER SAID I CANNOT LEAVE
THERE IS ANOTHER CHILD HERE
PLEASE TELL MY MUM
HE TOLD THEM TO KEEP TYPING
```

These fragments may be corrupted by lesson phrases:

```text
PLEASE TELL MY MUM I WILL NOT INTERRUPT
```

---

### Mr. Calder

Mr. Calder is the teacher connected to Emily’s disappearance.

He should not sound like a cartoon villain. His horror comes from calm, respectable, bureaucratic control.

His language:

```text
Emily struggles with attention.
Emily confuses distress with performance.
Please continue with the assigned exercise.
Do not reward disruption.
Good pupils finish their work.
Keep your eyes on the screen.
Do not turn around.
```

He represents adult authority, institutional denial, and the way official language can erase reality.

---

### Mr. Fingers

Mr. Fingers is the typing tutor mascot: a cheerful cartoon glove with eyes and a smile.

At first he is silly, encouraging, and educational. As the game progresses, he becomes paternal, coercive, wounded, and ambiguous.

He may represent:

- The original HOME ROW mascot
- The software’s instruction system
- Mr. Calder’s voice internalised
- Emily using the program to communicate
- The player’s repression mechanism
- A supernatural witness
- Some mixture of all of these

Important lines:

```text
Keep your eyes on the screen!
Good fingers stay where they belong.
You completed this lesson before.
You were so good that day.
Errors are not evidence.
I was trying to help you forget.
But she kept the file open.
```

Mr. Fingers should not be purely evil. He may be both protective and complicit.

---

## Core Mechanics as Story

Typing is not just input. It is obedience, memory, correction, and testimony.

### Key player behaviours

| Behaviour | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Perfect typing | Obedience / official version strengthens |
| Mistakes | Hidden truth leaks through |
| Backspace | Suppression / deletion / avoidance |
| Pauses | Audio or memory fragments surface |
| Rewriting | Player challenges the assigned sentence |
| Refusal | Player resists both truth and instruction |

### Suggested hidden stats

| Stat | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Obedience | How closely the player follows assigned lessons |
| Disclosure | How much hidden truth has surfaced through mistakes |
| Suppression | How often the player deletes, corrects, or avoids |
| Refusal | How often the player stops, pauses, or refuses |
| Witness Acceptance | Whether the player accepts partial memory as meaningful testimony |

## Story Structure

# Prologue — Boot Disk

## Scene

The player launches HOME ROW from an old desktop / disk / recovered school computer.

The fake desktop may include:

- `HOME_ROW.EXE`
- `README.TXT`
- `LESSONS`
- `STUDENT_RECORDS`
- `RECOVERY.LOG`

Only HOME ROW is initially available.

## Events

The program asks:

```text
Please enter your name.
```

The player enters any name.

The program responds:

```text
Name not found.
Typing pattern recognised.
Welcome back.
```

It briefly displays:

```text
Previous Session: 17 May 1997
Location: Computer Lab 2
Status: Incomplete
```

Then hides the information.

## Purpose

Establishes:

- The software knows the player.
- The past is stored in the system.
- The story is about an unfinished lesson.

---

# Act 1 — The Lesson Remembers

## Lesson 1: Home Row

### Surface drills

```text
ASDF JKL;
F J F J
DAD HAD A SAD LAD
A LAD HAD A DAD
```

The phrases begin as harmless typing nonsense but slowly feel meaningful.

### Hidden leaks

Mistakes briefly reveal words like:

```text
HELP
SHE
DOOR
LOCKED
```

### Mascot lines

```text
Mistakes are how we learn!
Keep your eyes on the screen!
Good fingers stay where they belong.
```

### Turning Point

At the end of the lesson:

```text
Accuracy: 94%
Memory Match: 41%
```

The player is not just being tested for typing. They are being matched.

---

# Act 2 — Emily Appears

## Lesson 2: Classroom Words

### Surface prompts

```text
DESK
CHAIR
BELL
REGISTER
ABSENT
LOCKED
AFTER SCHOOL
```

The UI subtly becomes more like a school computer lab. Rain appears at the windows.

### Student profile unlock

```text
STUDENT: EMILY VALE
CLASS: 3B
STATUS: ABSENT
NOTES: Disruptive / imaginative / requires firm boundaries
```

### Hidden corrections

The word `ABSENT` flickers into:

```text
REMOVED
RECORDED ABSENT
NOT ABSENT
```

### Teacher note

Official version:

```text
Emily has recently made troubling claims. Staff should avoid encouraging fantasy narratives.
```

Hidden version:

```text
Emily asked to call home.
Emily was told to finish the lesson first.
```

### Turning Point

The player believes the game is about Emily.

This is only half true.

---

# Act 3 — Obedience Training

## Lesson 3: Good Behaviour Sentences

### Surface prompts

```text
I WILL SIT STILL.
I WILL NOT INTERRUPT.
I WILL KEEP MY EYES ON THE SCREEN.
I WILL FINISH WHAT I START.
I WILL NOT MAKE STORIES.
```

### Compliance pressure

The program begins grading morally, not just mechanically.

| Player action | Program response |
|---|---|
| Perfect typing | Praise |
| Mistakes | Sharper correction |
| Backspace | Text smears / visual scars |
| Pauses | Audio memory fragments |
| Rewriting | Hostility |
| Refusal | Mascot pressure |

### First audio memory

During a pause, faint fragments may play:

- Chair scrape
- Rain
- Adult voice
- Keyboard tapping
- Child breathing

### Central sentence

```text
I WILL NOT INTERRUPT.
```

After typing it:

```text
You completed this lesson before.
```

### Turning Point

The program is replaying the player’s lesson, not just Emily’s.

---

# Act 4 — The Second Workstation

## Interlude: System Log

A hidden file opens:

```text
17 MAY 1997
4:12 PM — Emily Vale logged in
4:18 PM — Secondary workstation active
4:21 PM — HOME ROW Lesson 3 launched
4:31 PM — Door status: closed
4:34 PM — Teacher override enabled
4:47 PM — Input irregularity detected
4:48 PM — Secondary user idle

```

### Seating chart

A corrupted seating chart shows two active monitors.

One label:

```text
E. VALE
```

Second label:

```text
J_M_ / USER UNKNOWN / PATTERN MATCH 92%
```

Avoid forcing the player’s exact name unless needed. Ambiguity is stronger.

### Mascot lines

```text
Two pupils can learn quietly together.
One of them did.
```

### Turning Point

The player was not outside the mystery.

They were inside the room.

---

# Act 5 — Pronoun Shift

## Lesson 4: Dictation Mode

The program says:

```text
Please type what you hear.
```

But the displayed text and hidden text disagree.

### Prompt pairs

Displayed:

```text
THE ROOM WAS EMPTY.
```

Hidden:

```text
THE ROOM WAS LOCKED.
```

Displayed:

```text
THE TEACHER HAD LEFT.
```

Hidden:

```text
THE TEACHER HAD THE KEY.
```

Displayed:

```text
THE CHILD WAS DIFFICULT.
```

Hidden:

```text
THE CHILD WAS AFRAID.
```

### Pronoun shift

The prompts shift:

```text
EMILY DID NOT FINISH HER LESSON.
YOU DID NOT FINISH YOUR LESSON.
YOU HEARD HER CHAIR MOVE.
NO YOU DID NOT.
```

Memory and denial fight on the page.

### Mr. Calder’s commands

```text
KEEP TYPING.
DO NOT TURN AROUND.
GOOD CHILDREN DO NOT INTERRUPT.
SHE IS ONLY MAKING STORIES.
```

### Turning Point

The player starts remembering:

- They were seated nearby.
- Emily was behind or beside them.
- Something happened.
- They obeyed because they were a frightened child.

---

# Act 6 — Emily’s Attempt

## Lesson 5: Free Practice

The program unlocks a “free typing” exercise.

Emily’s recovered text appears in fragments:

```text
MY NAME IS EMILY VALE
I AM IN THE COMPUTER ROOM
MR CALDER SAID I CANNOT LEAVE
THERE IS ANOTHER CHILD HERE
PLEASE TELL MY MUM
HE TOLD THEM TO KEEP TYPING
```

Some lines are overwritten by lesson language:

```text
PLEASE TELL MY MUM I WILL NOT INTERRUPT
```

### Player choice

The player chooses how to handle Emily’s text:

1. Type it exactly as recovered
2. Correct it into official grammar
3. Delete uncertain parts
4. Add what they remember
5. Refuse to alter it

### Turning Point

The player is not just uncovering a file.

They are deciding whether to become a witness.

---

# Act 7 — The Program Protects / Attacks

## Mascot Break Scene

Mr. Fingers takes over the screen.

His face is too large. He is still smiling.

His dialogue changes based on the player’s dominant behaviour.

### If obedient

```text
You always were reliable.
You knew how to be safe.
```

### If error-heavy

```text
Careful. Errors become accusations.
```

### If rebellious

```text
You cannot improve what you abandoned.
```

### If refusal-heavy

```text
I held the lesson together when you could not.
```

### Key ambiguous reveal

```text
I was trying to help you forget.
```

Then later:

```text
But she kept the file open.
```

### Purpose

This is the ambiguity peak.

Mr. Fingers may be protecting the player from traumatic memory, suppressing Emily, preserving the lesson, or all of these.

---

# Act 8 — The Correction Exam

## Final lesson before ending

The program presents official statements with highlighted “errors.”

The player must correct, preserve, or rewrite.

### Example 1

```text
EMILY VALE LEFT SCHOOL VOLUNTARILY.
```

Highlighted word:

```text
VOLUNTARILY
```

Options:

- Delete it
- Replace with `SUDDENLY`
- Replace with `AFTER DETENTION`
- Leave it

### Example 2

```text
NO SECOND PUPIL WAS PRESENT.
```

Highlighted word:

```text
NO
```

Options:

- Delete `NO`
- Replace with `ONE`
- Replace with `I`
- Leave it

### Example 3

```text
THE WITNESS SAW NOTHING.
```

Highlighted word:

```text
SAW
```

Options:

- Replace with `HEARD`
- Replace with `REMEMBERED`
- Replace with `SAID`
- Leave it

### Purpose

Truth is not clean.

The player may not have seen everything. They did hear something. They were a child. Their statement matters anyway.

---

# Finale — Final Assessment

The interface strips away.

No mascot.  
No music.  
Only rain and the cursor blink.

The program displays:

```text
FINAL ASSESSMENT
Complete the sentence.
```

The sentence depends on prior play.

### Possible final prompts

Obedient route:

```text
I DID NOT SEE ANYTHING.
```

Disclosure route:

```text
I HEARD HER SAY NO.
```

Suppression route:

```text
I KEPT TYPING.
```

Rebellion route:

```text
I SHOULD HAVE TURNED AROUND.
```

Balanced / truthful route:

```text
I WAS A CHILD AND I WAS THERE.
```

The player can edit the sentence.

This is the climax.

## Endings

# 1. Gold Star Ending

## Conditions

High obedience, low errors, little rewriting.

## Outcome

The player completes the official lesson.

```text
NO ERRORS DETECTED
LESSON COMPLETE
```

Emily’s file remains:

```text
STATUS: ABSENT
```

Mr. Fingers says:

```text
Some children learn to move on.
```

Final image: a clean dot-matrix certificate:

```text
Certificate of Completion
```

Emily’s name appears faintly underneath.

## Meaning

The player remains protected by denial. Emily remains erased.

---

# 2. Error Report Ending

## Conditions

High mistakes / hidden text revealed, but low final agency.

## Outcome

The program crashes and exports an error report.

Fragments include:

```text
LOCKED ROOM
SECOND WORKSTATION
CALDER
KEEP TYPING
EMILY NOT ABSENT
```

Final line:

```text
Some errors should have been read sooner.
```

## Meaning

Truth surfaces, but remains fragmented.

---

# 3. Backspace Ending

## Conditions

Heavy deleting, correcting, suppressing.

## Outcome

The screen clears.

Then the first lesson restarts:

```text
ASDF JKL;
I DID NOT TURN AROUND
ASDF JKL;
I DID NOT TURN AROUND
```

Each loop has less colour.

Mr. Fingers says:

```text
Again. Neater this time.
```

## Meaning

Suppression becomes repetition.

---

# 4. Mascot Ending

## Conditions

High refusal / long pauses / refusal to complete final sentence.

## Outcome

Mr. Fingers types for the player.

At first:

```text
I DID NOT SEE ANYTHING.
```

Then he pauses, deletes it, and types:

```text
I WAS TRYING TO HELP YOU FORGET.
```

Then:

```text
BUT SHE KEPT THE FILE OPEN.
```

Final status:

```text
PROGRAM STILL RUNNING
```

## Meaning

Mr. Fingers is revealed as partly protective, partly complicit. The ambiguity remains.

---

# 5. Witness Statement Ending

## Conditions

The player reveals Emily’s text, accepts uncertainty, and writes a truthful final statement.

## Final sentence

Best version:

```text
I WAS A CHILD AND I WAS THERE.
```

The player may add:

```text
I HEARD HER.
I WAS TOLD NOT TO TURN AROUND.
I KEPT TYPING.
I REMEMBER NOW.
```

## Outcome

The program generates:

```text
RECOVERED WITNESS STATEMENT
SOURCE: HOME ROW SESSION ARCHIVE / USER MEMORY CACHE
RELIABILITY: PARTIAL
STATUS: RECORDED
```

Emily’s status changes:

```text
EMILY VALE: RECORDED
SECOND USER: REMEMBERED
```

## Meaning

Not saved. Not avenged. Just recorded.

Final line:

```text
The lesson did not end. It was only waiting for your hands.
```

## Cleanest Underlying Truth

The likely reality beneath the ambiguity:

Emily Vale tried to report something involving Mr. Calder. He kept her after school in the computer lab. The player was another child present at a second workstation. Calder told the player to keep typing and not look. Emily disappeared from the school record as “absent” or “transferred.” The player suppressed the memory. HOME ROW preserved enough data, rhythm, and language to make the player remember.

The supernatural layer does not replace the grounded event.

It exists because memory sometimes feels supernatural when it finally comes back.

---

## Development Tone Guide

HOME ROW should feel:

- Cheaply nostalgic at first
- Increasingly coercive
- Quietly horrifying rather than jump-scare driven
- Institutional, not demonic
- Emotionally restrained
- Ambiguous but not vague
- About testimony, memory, and correction

Avoid making the story too literal too early. The player should spend much of the game unsure whether the program is haunted, corrupted, or acting as their own subconscious.

The ending should not offer clean justice. The strongest resolution is partial recording: Emily is no longer absent, and the player is no longer pretending they were never there.
